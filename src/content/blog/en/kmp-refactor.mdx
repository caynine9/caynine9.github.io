In the startup world, the pace is often compared to a Formula 1 race. It's really fast, and requiring decisions to be made in a heartbeat. 

I recently found myself in one of those "seize the moment" scenarios. My native Android app was live and stable, when a key stakeholder arrived with a familiar request: **they needed iOS support, and they needed it fast**. Instead of falling into a panic, I viewed it as an opportunity to transition the project to **Kotlin Multiplatform (KMP)**.

I've been delving deep into KMP for several months before this, and to me this seems like it could be a great way for me to test out the waters.

### Setting the Stage: Scoping and Alignment

Before a single line of code was changed, I realized that the most important work happened outside the IDE. In a rapid-fire environment, you have to align your vision with the stakeholders immediately. I spent time defining exactly what needed to be moved and managing expectations regarding the development cycle. 

It wasn't about porting the entire UI, but rather it was about isolating the core business logic so it could live in a shared module.

This clarity prevented the "scope creep" that usually kills short sprints.

### Playing Detective: Understanding the Flow

Once the scope was set, I had to become a detective within my own codebase. I spent time mapping the data flow from the API and database all the way to the UI layer. I had to identify every platform-specific dependency. For example, stuff like `SharedPreferences` or Android-specific `Context` calls. These were the parts that would eventually need to be abstracted using KMP’s `expect/actual` mechanism.

### The Secret Weapon: AI-Driven Planning

The real "X Factor" in making a 4-day timeline work was how I integrated AI into the workflow. Rather than just asking an AI to write code, I used it in "Plan Mode" to iterate on the refactor strategy before execution. By defining technical specs early, I saved hours of potential headaches. 

I used the AI to help map out module boundaries and then relied on more cost-effective models to handle the repetitive boilerplate work once the architectural foundation was solid. 

With how pricing models for AI subscriptions has just changed (example for GitHub Copilot, see [here](https://github.blog/news-insights/company-news/github-copilot-is-moving-to-usage-based-billing/), and [here](https://docs.github.com/en/copilot/reference/copilot-billing/models-and-pricing#model-multipliers-for-annual-copilot-pro-and-copilot-pro-subscribers)), this could be the most cost efficient way you can reduce token usage without sacrificing output quality.

### The 4-Day Sprint

The sprint itself was a blur of focused execution. The first day was dedicated entirely to infrastructure: Configuring `build.gradle.kts` and setting up the `commonMain` folders. By the second day, the focus shifted to the data layer, swapping out Android-only networking for **Ktor**. 

The third day was the most intense, as I migrated the business logic and repositories into the shared module, carefully abstracting platform-specific functions. Finally, on the fourth day, I integrated the shared framework into Xcode. Seeing the logic run on a basic SwiftUI screen for the first time was the ultimate validation of the KMP approach.

### Reflections for the Road
Looking back, this transition taught me that successful refactoring is less about typing speed and more about planning. If you're attempting this, prioritize "KMP-First" libraries like **Koin** for dependency injection or **Ktor** for networking client. 

And also, don't be afraid to modularize early; keeping your data, domain, and feature logic separate will save you from a "shared module" that becomes a junk drawer. 

Most importantly, remember to handle your `Coroutines` with care. Android dispatchers don't always behave the same way on iOS, so using a clean wrapper for Swift’s `async/await` is essential for a professional finish. 

Refactoring to KMP is a challenge, but in a fast-moving market, it’s one of the best ways to ensure your app is ready for whatever comes next.
